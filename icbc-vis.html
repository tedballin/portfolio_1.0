ICBC Visual Analytics

The Project

This data visualization project is designed to help explain the composition of ICBC claims costs in terms of collision frequency and types in Lower Mainland. Our interest in this topic is to further discover how property damage only (PDO) and casualty collisions contribute to the huge claims incurred operating costs that lead to ICBC's financial burden. The final visualizations could be used as a reference for future decision making for government officials and ICBC staff members.

Strategy:

Knowing the limited time and data resources we have, we narrowed the scope of the project and focused on the claims incurred costs. By asking problem questions such as: Is ICBC paying more for PDO or for injury claim? Do car crashes in each city have its own pattern depends on collision types? We searched categorical, ordinal, and quantitative data in order to generate visualizations that might help domain experts to solve the financial crisis.

Data Wrangling:

One of the biggest challenges was data cleaning. Since we need the data of collision types, accident count, collision locations and operating costs, ICBC became our major data resource. In the Lower Mainland crashes dataset, we removed the unused dimensions and filtered key information by programming. For information like operating costs, ICBC does not offer open dataset; we had to look through its financial report and manually create the dataset in the .csv file.

Visualization Design:

I was mainly responsible for designing and implementing visualizations using JavaScript and D3.js library. For the key visualizations, I used a choropleth map to provide an overview of the collision frequency in Lower Mainland area, and also it allows the audiences to hover over the cities and look into the details. The categorical data and cities are showed on the map along with the quantitative crash count data.

I also created hexbin map to provide more detailed information on car accidents in each city combine such as accident distributions (street, latitude and longitude) and collision types by hovering over each spot. The two crash types as categorical attributes are differentiated by color hue in small pie charts.

The Interaction:

In addition to the interaction with individual visualizations. I also created coordinated interaction between visualizations that allow the users to interpret and explore more as needed. The bar chart encodes two attributes using a line mark with the vertical spatial position channel for the quantitative attribute, the claim incurred costs and the horizontal spatial position channel for the categorical attribute, major cities in Lower Mainland. The hexbin map will update accordingly when users filter the cities and collision types in the bar chart by clicking on each bar, so as to see the dominant type of car accidents reported in each city.

Reflection

ICBC visual analytics was a challenging project. In order to create the ideal visualizations, I searched and took inspirations from some of Mike Bostock's examples. It was quite challenging as I am still new to the D3 library and some changes of syntax between the old and new versions. I would like to implement the coordinated interaction between the historical trends of accidents and choropleth map in the future.

This project really allowed me to put together different types of skills and learn a lot during the process. Through the project, I learned the intricacies of managing, working with data and transforming it into useful and readable information.
