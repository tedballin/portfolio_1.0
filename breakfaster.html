Breakfaster

The Project

Breakfaster is a web (mobile friendly) application that centralizes and streamlines administrative tasks for Breakfast Club of Canada staff, school breakfast program coordinators, and volunteers. It helps users to plan and prepare breakfast meals, browse and create recipes, and order ingredients for future meals.

Type：

UX Design
Non-profeit Project

Tools：

Foundational Research:

After the first meeting with the client (Breakfast Club of Canada), we knew the application is more than a simple combination of recipe and meal planning tools. We started our foundational research in order to understand the landscape of the project and uncover insights into the users' behaviours, needs, and goals. By conducting user interviews with Breakfast Club staff, both urban and rural school staff (teachers facilitating the breakfast program), and executives of Langley School District Foundation (partner of Breakfast Club of Canada), we learned the interviewees’ daily work routines, challenges encountered, technology usage, and context of use.

The Problems:

By conducting the these user interviews, we defined several issues: School coordinators often create weekly menu, track inventory and make orders for ingredients without using any technical tools, these type of mental work are difficult for students and volunteers as they are unable to keep track of all the details and help to ease the workload; school coordinators are also seeking some flexibility in terms of food ingredients as local supplies might vary. Executives of local school district foundation mentioned the difficulty of maintaining breakfast variety which ultimately related to funding. The staff at BCC concerned that whether schools follow the recommended ingredient list and make nutritious breakfast for students.

Strategy:

We used several UX methods such as user story mapping, persona, and user journey in order to explore the pain points, synthesize findings, and come up with some ideas. We envisioned an all-in-one platform is needed to help users to plan and prepare breakfast meals by browsing and adding recipes, track current inventory and expense, and order ingredients for future meals.

We mapped out user stories for both school and BCC staff, the goals were to understand the overall flow, find out obstacles they are experiencing, and identify pain points or design opportunities. We focused our efforts on understanding the phases of the plan, order, and coordinate. The application or solution should be convenience and resourceful that helps to offload extensive information for different users.

Exploration:

With the basic understandings of the project and our target audience‘s needs, I sketched dozens of wireframes for both desktop and mobile views in order to visualize and validate our ideas quickly. After an initial discussion with my partner in the team, I reiterated the sketches and created digital wireframes in Figma. In addition to wireframes, I also built a sitemap and shared them with our developers. Based on the wireframes and sitemap, the team discussed key features, app flow for the public, school, and BCC users, initial database design, as well as technical feasibilities and options.

Current Proposal

The project is still in process currently, I created most high-fidelity interactive mocks and we have sent our design proposal to the client for approval. Some key features for fulfilling users’ needs include: recipe feature that saves healthy breakfast recipes recommended by BCC or custom recipes; menu feature that allows users to add selected recipes and generate weekly menu, pantry feature that helps to track food inventory; order feature that enables user to order ingredients based on future weekly menu and current inventory.

Challenges

Designing the mobile view was one of the biggest challenges I encountered. Because of the features needed and multi-level of administrations involved, the user interface of the application is relatively complex, and that is also the reason we decided to build a web instead of a mobile application. At the same time, we need to focus on mobile responsive design to fit the usage context of the target audience, since users might use it in the kitchen or food storage room. I used mobile-first approach to prioritize most essential and frequently used features since the screen real estate on the mobile device is so limited. During the design process, the borderline between mobile app interface and mobile site interface got blurred, it seems natural for us to follow design conventions that commonly used in mobile apps, but these design conventions do not always apply to the mobile site. For example, turning the control panel into tab bar at the bottom causes visual inconsistency between mobile and desktop view, and users who resizing the browser window on a mobile device (tablet) might find the transition is not intuitive. Instead, I used tab bar for major features at the top as well as utility features like print, bookmark, and share.

Reflection

The project is challenging yet captivating, as it is my first-time work on an end-to-end project in the real world. I learned to be fluid working with my team members as well as different stakeholders. Currently, our project is on hold due to other factors such as technical capabilities and maintenance concerns on the client’s side, but I am confident to iterate under different circumstances and deliver a convenience and resourceful tool to the end users.
